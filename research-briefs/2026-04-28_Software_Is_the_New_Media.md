## The 90-Second Frame

The panel question is the entire frame: *"Now that AI can code and generate apps, is the line between software and content/media companies becoming blurry?"* The lineup is engineered to answer it from three opposing positions — and that's the whole reason the conversation will be interesting.

- **Dan Shipper** runs the company that lives the question literally. Every IS a media company (subscription writing collective, podcast network) AND a software company (Every Studio: Spiral, Sparkle, Lex, Cora). They publish *and* they ship product. Whatever framework Dan offers will be earned, not theorized.
- **Michael Rothman** runs the company that the question challenges. The Dispatch is "fact-based journalism from a center-right perspective" — a 2019-founded subscription publication, just made him **its first president in January 2026**. Rothman's prior life was Fatherly (co-founder) and Thrillist (7 years building digital sales) — meaning he is exactly the kind of digital-media-ad-era veteran the new AI era is supposed to render obsolete. His pivot to subscription journalism is itself a take on the question.
- **Iris ten Teije** doesn't run a media company at all. She runs an *infrastructure* company (Sky Valley Ambient Computing) building the substrate for software that evolves at runtime. Her presence on this panel signals that Borthwick reads "software is the new media" through an OS-layer lens — software you watch and software that watches you back are both about to be constantly evolving artifacts.
- **John Borthwick** is the right host. Betaworks has been thesis-investing in the AI/media intersection for a decade (BotCamp 2016 → augmentative AI → Spring 2026 Camp on **Agent Systems**, which is *this same week*). The Dispatch, Every, and Sky Valley are not random panelists — they are three vertices of Borthwick's portfolio thesis.

The room itself is signal. **Two consecutive Betaworks evenings** this week (Mon Shortlist + Tue panel) is not an accident — Borthwick uses the venue to convene the operators he wants in his orbit. Worth noting in the post.

## Topic 1: The Three Vertices on the Panel

### 1.1 Dan Shipper — **Every** (and Every Studio)

**The artifact:** Every is two things stacked.

1. **A media company** — subscription writing collective, podcast network ("AI & I" hosted by Dan, "Chain of Thought" column also by Dan). Subscriber-funded, no ads, no SEO play.
2. **A software studio** — Every Studio incubates and launches AI products. Current lineup as of April 2026:
   - **Spiral v3** (just shipped) — "AI writing partner with taste." Rebuilt with editorial sensibility from Every's own writing canon. $25/mo standalone OR free for paid Every subscribers. The most interesting positioning move: bundling software *into* the media subscription, inverting how SaaS pricing usually works.
   - **Sparkle v4** — AI desktop organizer that examines your filesystem and proposes structure tailored to how you work.
   - **Lex** — older AI writing tool, still in market.
   - **Cora** — unannounced, in development.

**The thesis (Dan's):** Software and media companies are converging *because the same writing-and-editing skills* that make Every's content sharp are what make their software opinionated. He's been writing about this in Every's "Master Plan: Part II" piece.

**Why he matters for the panel question:** Dan's answer to "is the line blurring?" will be empirical, not speculative. He has the P&L of a media company AND a SaaS to compare. Listen for whether he claims the bundle works (Spiral free for subscribers) or whether he admits the subscriber-LTV math has been the load-bearing piece.

**For Alex specifically:** Every is the tightest peer Alex has in the documentarian/AI/independent-media space — but at scale and with software revenue underneath. The model Alex is building (events pipeline + content engine + portfolio of small AI projects) maps to Every's playbook with about 18 months of runway compression. **Dan is the panelist Alex should most want a real conversation with.**

### 1.2 Michael Rothman — **The Dispatch**

**The artifact:** The Dispatch is a 2019-founded subscription journalism publication — "fact-based journalism from a center-right perspective for independent thinkers." Founded by Steve Hayes and Jonah Goldberg (both ex-National Review / ex-Weekly Standard). Rothman was a company advisor since inception, joined full-time in 2024, named **first president in January 2026.**

**Rothman's career arc (the panel-relevant part):** Co-founder of **Fatherly** (digital lifestyle brand for fathers). Founding employee at **Thrillist** — built digital sales and revenue ops for the first 7 years. Career-long digital-media operator. Then he pivots to subscription-funded fact-based journalism in 2024-2026. **That arc is the entire commentary on the panel question.**

**What he's done at The Dispatch in his 4-month tenure:**
- Spearheaded revenue diversification (away from pure subscription).
- Completed The Dispatch's first acquisition (**SCOTUSBlog**, 2024) — buying earned audience + niche topical authority.
- Public talk planned with HBSCNY: *"Fact-Based Media in an AI Era"* — that's literally the panel topic from his angle.

**Why he matters for the panel question:** Rothman is going to argue the human-first position — *"AI-first" is incompatible with credibility-first journalism*. He'll likely cite SCOTUSBlog as evidence that earned subject-matter expertise (something AI cannot fake) is the moat. Watch for whether he concedes that AI-augmented production is fine, just not AI-replacement.

**For Alex specifically:** The Dispatch is *the* clearest articulation of "the human-first business" the panel question asks about. Rothman's playbook (subscription + acquisitions + earned authority) is a model for how independent media survives — and Alex is, structurally, building independent media (LinkedIn presence, event coverage). Rothman is the panelist most likely to give Alex a hard, useful framework rather than a hot take.

### 1.3 Iris ten Teije — **Sky Valley Ambient Computing**

**The artifact:** Sky Valley is building infrastructure for **adaptive software** — software that "remains as fluid after deployment as it is during creation, evolving continuously at runtime as users interact with it." Their products:
- **Differ** — adaptive runtime platform. Drop your app in, it starts evolving in production as agents observe user behavior, infer intent, and edit + redeploy autonomously.
- **Intent Space** — open-source protocol for agent coordination. Agents broadcast intent to a shared log; other agents self-select what to work on (no orchestrator). **This is the same primitive Thursday's hackathon is built around.**

**Founding team:** Iris ten Teije and Noam Tenne. Iris's public posts focus on **adaptive UIs that evolve in real time** and **what happens to UIs in the age of agents** — directly on the panel topic from the infrastructure side.

**Cross-event linkage:** Sky Valley appears in TWO events this week — this Tuesday panel (Iris speaking) AND **Thursday's Multi-Agent Hackathon** (Sky Valley co-hosting with Tribute Labs). Same company, same week, two different formats. Worth flagging in the Sunday roundup as "company to watch."

**Why she matters for the panel question:** Iris will reframe the question. The other two will debate "is software becoming media?" — Iris's answer is closer to *"both are becoming the same thing: artifacts that update themselves in real time based on user behavior."* That's the OS-layer view that makes the panel substantive instead of two-sided.

**For Alex specifically:** Iris is the panelist with the *least* overlap to Alex's GTM background but the *most* relevance to the agentic-systems trend Alex is documenting. Engaging her well at the mixer means asking infrastructure-level questions, not GTM-level ones.

## Topic 3: Tailwinds — The Macro Behind "Software Is the New Media"

The panel question is timely because the underlying market just crossed several thresholds in 2026:

- **$91.57B generative AI market** in 2026 (up from ~$40B in 2024).
- **900M ChatGPT weekly active users** (more than the daily-active count of any single news publication).
- **$300B VC into AI startups in Q1 2026 alone.** That's not a typo — one quarter.
- **AI video production cost down 91%** vs. traditional ($4,500/minute → $400/minute).
- **67% of brands** use AI-generated video for at least some social content; **75%** expected by end of 2026.
- **A16z just published** "The State of Generative Media 2026" — their headline thesis is that platforms controlling generation + distribution + monetization win the next decade.

The collapse of the cost-of-content curve is the real story. When making content is 10–100× cheaper, *distribution* and *taste* become the only scarce resources. That's where The Dispatch's "earned authority" play, Every's "editorial taste embedded in software" play, and Sky Valley's "software that adapts to the user" play all converge.

**For NYC specifically:** Betaworks is one of the only firms in the country with a 10-year continuous AI/media thesis. SF has more capital deployed in pure-AI infra. NYC's edge is the AI/media intersection — and this panel is the curated articulation of that edge.

## Topic 4: Top Questions Worth Asking in the Room

1. **"You bundled Spiral v3 free into the Every subscription. What did you learn from the early data — does it lift subscriber LTV enough to justify the standalone revenue you're forgoing? Or is it the other way around: subscribers are paying for the software and the writing is the bonus?"** *(P&L-shaped question. Shows you understand the model. Forces a real number from Dan, not a vibe.)*

### For Michael Rothman (The Dispatch)
3. **"Your career is Fatherly + Thrillist + The Dispatch — the arc from ad-driven digital media to subscription fact-based journalism. What did you learn at Thrillist and Fatherly about ad-driven media that made you bet on subscription as the only viable model in the AI era?"** *(Career-arc question. Shows you read his bio. Will get a real answer because you've handed him the framework.)*

## Topic 5: Documentarian Angle (for Content)

The strongest angles for Alex's audience — ranked by sharpness.

### Angle A (sharpest): "Three companies, three models for the human-first business in an AI-first world."

The panel lineup IS the content. Every (subscription + product), The Dispatch (subscription + acquired authority), Sky Valley (infrastructure that adapts). All three are answers to the same question. Walk through what each model actually requires (taste, authority, attention to user behavior) and where they overlap.

**Hook:** *"Three companies on tonight's panel each have a different answer to the same question: how do you build a human-first business in an AI-first world? Not theoretically — operationally, with revenue, cap tables, and customer counts to back the answer up."*

**Why it works:** Specific, observable, fair to all three panelists, surfaces a useful framework, demonstrates you actually paid attention. Best for the post-event recap, but Alex can foreshadow this frame in the pre-event post.

### Angle B: "The cost of content fell 91%. Distribution and taste became the only scarce resources."

Pulls the macro frame (A16z State of Generative Media, AI video cost collapse, 900M ChatGPT WAU) and shows that the panel is asking the right question because the underlying economics shifted. Use specific numbers from the macro topic.

**Hook:** *"AI cut video production costs from $4,500/minute to $400/minute. 75% of marketing video will be AI-generated by end of 2026. When making content goes nearly free, only two things stay scarce: distribution and taste. Tonight's panel at Betaworks is three companies betting on one or the other."*

**Why it works:** Anchors in numbers that pass the expert-pause test. Documentarian voice (analyst). Connects an event observation to the broader market shift.

### Angle C: "The most interesting subscription business in indie media is also a software studio."

Goes narrow on Every. Spiral v3 free for paid subscribers is a deliberate inversion of how SaaS pricing works — bundle the software *into* the media product, not the other way around. Worth a standalone post if Dan gives a sharp data point.

**Hook:** *"Spiral v3 is $25/month standalone. Or free if you subscribe to Every's writing. Every is doing what nobody else in indie media has tried — bundling the software into the subscription instead of bolting it on. The early data is going to determine whether the indie-media playbook of the next 5 years comes from journalism or from product."*

**Why it works:** Specific company, specific number, specific bet. Best for a standalone post if Dan gives Alex a real data point at the mixer (otherwise stays speculative).

**Sky Valley appearing in TWO events this week** (Tue panel + Thu hackathon) is itself the pattern.
